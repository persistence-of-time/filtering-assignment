What is the difference between misinformation and disinformation?

Language barriers encourage the spread of false information in Spanish-speaking communities.
This story was originally produced in Spanish.

Misinformation and disinformation in spanish speaking communities is a serious problem that has lead to consequences. Illustration by: Gustavo Garcia
By: Anacaona Rodriguez Martinez, Lucy Cabrera, Gustavo Garcia
Published: Mar 22, 2022 8:00 AM

Like COVID-19, misinformation and disinformation also spread like a virus among the most vulnerable communities more commonly behind a cell phone or computer screen through social networks. And with vaccines, misinformation has caused many people to decide not to get vaccinated against COVID-19.

Liz Lebrón, research manager for the Latino Anti-Disinformation Laboratory, a partnership between Voto Latino and Media Matters for America, explained the difference between misinformation and disinformation.

The intention makes the difference:

Misinformation is false information that is innocently shared that appeals to the things we value and is spread in familiar spaces like WhatsApp.

“There are people who are genuinely misinformed. There are people who just have that information and share it, and they're not necessarily bad actors. This is misinformation,” Lebròn said.

Disinformation is false information that has been deliberately created with the intent to cause harm. For example, Russia created a campaign of this type of content during the 2016 presidential elections to destabilize and create chaos using robot accounts on twitter.

“Disinformation spreaders are very sophisticated. You don't see absolutely false narratives that are so easy to refute," Lebròn explained.

Would you be able to identify truthful information?

The elDetector is a project that was created specifically to fight problematic information in Spanish speaking communities. It is the first fact checking project of its kind in spanish.
